# Cincinnati Civic Landscape: Research Report
**Prepared:** March 2026 | **For:** Cincinnati Civic Data Platform

---

## Executive Summary

Cincinnati stands at a genuine inflection point. The 2001 riots catalyzed national-model police reform, but 25 years later, profound racial and economic disparities persist — and in some dimensions are worsening. The city reversed a 60-year population decline in 2020, but growth is suburban-led and urban neighborhoods face simultaneous disinvestment and gentrification pressures. Climate risk is escalating. School funding is in structural crisis. And yet: there is real momentum. A major zoning reform passed in 2024. An AI strategy called Ascend 2030 positions Cincinnati for smart-city leadership. GE Aerospace is expanding. BRT construction on two corridors is beginning.

For a civic data platform, the core opportunity is this: **the tradeoffs are real and the public deserves to see them**. Zoning reform vs. displacement risk. Transit investment vs. equity of access. Flood protection vs. riverfront development. Police accountability vs. community trust. The data exists. The challenge is synthesis, accessibility, and accountability.

## 1. Historical Context

### The 2001 Riots and Police Accountability Legacy

The April 2001 riots erupted after CPD killed 15 Black males between 1995 and 2001 — four in the months immediately preceding the unrest. Timothy Thomas, unarmed and 19, was the final killing. The DOJ investigation resulted in a 2002 Memorandum of Agreement mandating sweeping reforms: revised use-of-force documentation, crisis intervention training, improved supervision. The decree was lifted in 2007 and is widely regarded as one of the most successful early DOJ consent decrees — though questions about cultural persistence after oversight ended remain open.

**Platform relevance:** Our Police Accountability tab exists because of this legacy. The CPD traffic/pedestrian stop data and use-of-force data are its direct continuation. The accountability infrastructure is now entirely self-governed, which raises the stakes for public visibility of that data.

### Over-the-Rhine Gentrification (2000s–Present)

OTR transformed from Cincinnati's most disinvested neighborhood into a nationally-recognized revival district. Median prices now exceed $300k. Housing prices rose 20.9% year-over-year through 2024. Yet 34.3% of remaining residents live in poverty, 36% receive SNAP, and 70% of those served by Our Daily Bread (2023) were homeless. Community-based housing developers have preserved 400+ subsidized units — far short of what displacement demands.

**Platform relevance:** OTR is the template for what our Displacement Pressure Index is designed to detect *early* — before a neighborhood becomes the next OTR story. Avondale, Walnut Hills, and Price Hill are showing early signals.

### Demographic Shifts

Cincinnati reversed a 60-year population decline. The 2020 Census recorded 309,317 residents (+4.2% from 2010), the first net growth since the 1950 Census. 2025 estimate: 314,915. But the growth story is bifurcated: Boone County grew 6.9%, Warren County 6.1%, while Hamilton County grew only 0.9%. Suburban sprawl is continuing despite urban recovery.

## 2. Current Focus Areas (2023–2026)

### Housing Affordability & Evictions

- The housing wage is **$22.98/hour** — the minimum to afford a modest 2BR apartment in Cincinnati
- Ohio landlords filed nearly **108,000 eviction cases in 2024**, more than any year since 2015
- Cincinnati's eviction rate (~9% of renters) is above the national average
- Only 7% of tenants have legal representation vs. 93% of landlords in housing court
- Only **32 affordable units exist per 100 residents** earning below $35,000/year

**Recent policy responses:**
- **Connected Communities zoning reform** (adopted June 5, 2024; effective July 1, 2024) removes minimum lot sizes, parking mandates, and enables missing middle housing across most neighborhoods
- **Affordable Housing Leverage Fund:** $74.3M committed to 54 projects (Sept 2022–Sept 2024), creating/preserving 1,907 units (1,803 income-restricted)
- **Federal Home Loan Bank of Cincinnati:** $49.5M in Affordable Housing Program grants — the largest in the bank's history — to create 2,743 units across four states

### Police Accountability

With the DOJ consent decree lifted in 2007, CPD is self-governed. Modern accountability relies on public data transparency and community oversight. Recent innovations include a policy to send **unarmed mental health professionals** to some nonviolent 911 calls. The data our platform tracks (traffic stops by race, use-of-force, OIS incidents) remains the primary public window into CPD accountability.

### School Funding Crisis

Cincinnati Public Schools faces a structural budget emergency:
- Bracing for a **>$50M budget gap** as Ohio lawmakers considered significant K-12 funding cuts
- Lost $5M+ in federal funds (July 2024) that supported 37 FTE positions
- Receives only $0.36 per dollar per student under the revised state funding formula
- Cuts include teaching positions, assistant principals, and halved nursing staff
- This is a **racial equity crisis**: CPS is majority Black and lower-income; the funding formula structurally disadvantages urban districts

### Environmental Justice & Lead

**Lead service lines:**
- Cincinnati inventoried **52,129 lead or unknown service lines** in 2017
- As of 2025, **33,449 active/unknown lines remain** — 64% of the original inventory
- City aims to phase out all lead lines by 2032
- Average **220 cases/year** of elevated blood lead levels in Cincinnati children (2015–2024)
- Only **36.8% of Cincinnati children are tested annually**
- Hamilton County ranked **54th of Ohio's 88 counties** for lead-poisoned children (2024)

**Industrial contamination:**
- City completed nearly 40 Phase I environmental site assessments (2023 Green Cincinnati Plan)
- EPA awarded $75k to Groundwork Ohio River Valley for air quality monitoring in Lower Price Hill
- Cincinnati Port received $800k EPA grant + $16M in state cleanup grants for industrial site remediation
- The Groundwork Ohio River Valley Ecodistrict project (Lower Price Hill) targets flooding, urban heat, and air pollution through green infrastructure

### Transit (SORTA)

The **Reinventing Metro** initiative is the most significant transit investment in Cincinnati in decades:
- **Bus Rapid Transit (BRT) approved** for Hamilton Avenue and Reading Road corridors; construction planned to begin in 2025
- **MetroNow!** (mobility-on-demand service) launched May 2023; expanding to additional zones
- 4,000+ bus stop signs updated across the network; real-time signage improvements underway

### Racial Equity

The **Urban League's "State of Black Cincinnati" report (June 2024)** reveals stark, persistent disparities:
- **35.6% of Black residents live in poverty** vs. 16.5% of white residents
- **Median household income:** $31,520 for Black families vs. $70,909 for white families
- **Mortgage approval rates:** 67% for white borrowers vs. 17.5% for Black borrowers (2020)
- Black residents face significantly higher criminal justice system involvement

**All-In Cincinnati** (2019–present) is a community-driven racial equity and economic mobility initiative achieving measurable progress on pay equity, housing, and broadband access. Their co-governance model is now scaling nationally.

### Major Development Projects

- **Brent Spence Bridge replacement** ($3.6B, 8-mile corridor): A companion bridge is being built alongside the existing structure. Major construction ramps through 2026, completion ~2030. This is the largest infrastructure investment in Cincinnati's modern era.
- **FC Cincinnati Mixed-Use District** ($300M, 8.5 acres north of TQL Stadium): Hotel, apartments, office, retail. First buildings expected end of 2025; full buildout 7–8 years.
- **New arena feasibility study** (November 2024): 18,000-seat arena at $675–800M, two preferred sites in the West End or near Duke Energy Convention Center.

## 3. Climate Resilience & The 10–15 Year Outlook (2026–2040)

### Flood Risk Is Accelerating

- On **April 7, 2025**, the Ohio River reached 60 feet in Cincinnati — its highest level in seven years
- About **14% of Cincinnati buildings** are at flood risk, averaging a 41% chance of flooding (~2.9 feet deep) over 30 years
- Flooding patterns that historically occurred once every 20 years now occur every **5–10 years**
- The Cincinnati Local Flood Protection Project (Mill Creek Barrier Dam, 14 floodgates, 1.5-mile floodwall) was built in **1948** and is nearly 90 years old — urgently needs upgrading

### AI & Smart City Trajectory

- **Ascend 2030** (City + Hamilton County + UC + P&G + Microsoft): AI-driven city services, economic opportunity, civic data access. Codified in Cincinnati AI Blueprint v3.0, targeting 12 operational domains
- **Cincy AI Week** (June 2025): 2,000+ participants; tracks including "AI for Government & Smart Cities"
- **Intelligent Transportation System (ITS) upgrade** (March 2026): Actelis Networks selected for hybrid fiber-copper networking + AI-based cyber monitoring — signals real-time traffic data improvements coming
- **UC Innovation District** ($100M invested): Digital Futures + 1819 Innovation Hub, pioneering AI ethics and governance research

### Regional Economic Anchors

Cincinnati's $157B real GDP outpaces Cleveland, Columbus, and Indianapolis. Three Fortune 500 HQs:
- Kroger (Rank 27)
- Procter & Gamble (Rank 51)
- GE Aerospace (Rank 118; standalone since April 2024)

**GE Aerospace** announced $100M+ investment in southwest Ohio facilities (2025), driving advanced manufacturing and aerospace job growth between Cincinnati and Dayton.

### 10–15 Year Structural Trends

1. **Housing pressure deepening** — Unless policy changes, displacement in gentrifying corridors will accelerate. The Connected Communities zoning reform may increase supply but lacks affordability mandates.

2. **Climate infrastructure investment** — Brent Spence (2026–2030) is the largest project. Mill Creek flood protection upgrades will follow. Green infrastructure projects (Lower Price Hill) will scale.

3. **AI integration across city services** — Ascend 2030 matures. Predictive services improve. Equity concerns around algorithmic governance will intensify and need public scrutiny.

4. **Regional transit evolution** — BRT on Hamilton/Reading demonstrates demand; expect expansion to other corridors. SORTA's MetroNow! scales. Regional rail (Cincinnati–Columbus, Cincinnati–Dayton) conversations may emerge by 2035.

5. **Racial equity: slow but measurable gains** — All-In Cincinnati's co-governance model influences policy. Income and homeownership disparities narrow incrementally. Reparations discussions (Truth & Equity Initiative) mature into policy.

6. **Post-industrial remediation** — 40+ contaminated industrial sites remediate over 15 years, many converting to parks/green spaces or mixed-use development (already happening along the riverfront).

7. **School transformation** — Structural funding crisis forces innovation in STEM/CTE and school consolidation. GE Aerospace and P&G talent demands shape curriculum. CPS may consolidate or restructure significantly by 2035.

8. **Demographic stabilization** — City population holds at 315–325k. Suburban growth continues but slows as housing costs rise. Immigration trends (2025 restrictions) suppress regional growth. By 2035, urban-suburban transit connectivity will determine talent retention.

## 4. Civic Data Landscape

### What Cincinnati Has

- **Cincinnati Open Data Portal** (data.cincinnati-oh.gov): Strong Socrata-based infrastructure; 200+ datasets covering crime, permits, inspections, tax abatements, zoning, transit, health, and more
- **CincyInsights**: Visual dashboard portal showing key city performance metrics
- **OPDA (Office of Performance & Data Analytics)**: Manages open data, CincyInsights, and annual Performance Management Agreements with city departments
- **CAGIS**: Hamilton County GIS infrastructure with parcel, parks, neighborhood boundary, and environmental data

### Key Data Gaps

| Gap | Priority | Notes |
|-----|----------|-------|
| Lead service line replacement progress | **CRITICAL** | City publishes data but no civic-facing map exists |
| Hamilton County eviction court filings | High | Eviction Lab has county totals; granular data needs partner |
| Real-time SORTA ridership/performance | High | GTFS static only; dynamic data limited |
| Racial equity metrics by neighborhood | High | Urban League report reveals need; open data exists via Census |
| CPS school performance by attendance zone | High | ESSA report cards not machine-readable |
| Affordable housing unit inventory + expiration | Medium | HUD public API available |
| 311 service request response times by neighborhood | Medium | Data exists on Cincinnati open data portal |
| Environmental contamination remediation status | Medium | EPA Brownfields database has some; city data incomplete |
| BRT construction progress/impact | Medium | SORTA publishes info but not structured data |
| Connected Communities zoning impact | Medium | Pre/post analysis would be valuable |

### What Peer Cities Do Better

- **Baltimore**: Real-time 311 complaint data + neighborhood health disparities dashboard
- **Philadelphia**: Affordable housing supply/demand tracker (OpenDataPhilly)
- **Seattle/Washington**: CalEnviroScreen-style environmental justice cumulative impact maps
- **New York**: PLUTO property database — comprehensive ownership, land use, tax records at parcel level
- **Denver**: Flood risk mapper integrated with municipal GIS

## 6. Implications for the Platform

The research confirms the platform's core thesis — and reveals three significant gaps in coverage:

**What the research validates (we're tracking the right things):**
- Police accountability (CPD stop data by race is essential civic infrastructure)
- Displacement pressure (housing + permit + abatement data is exactly right)
- Neighborhood profiles (crime, permits, food safety, fire incidents)
- Flood risk (FEMA data in Explorer; Ohio River flooding is accelerating)
- Accessibility (Session 6 addition aligned with city priorities)

**Critical gaps the research reveals:**

1. **Lead & Environmental Health** — Cincinnati's lead crisis (33k+ service lines, 220 child cases/year) has no civic-facing map. This is arguably the most urgent *addressable* public health data gap. The city has the data; we're not surfacing it.

2. **Racial Equity Dashboard** — The Urban League's data is compelling and Cincinnati-specific. The platform has race data in police stops but no dedicated equity lens across housing, income, health, and criminal justice. This is a gap between what residents need and what we provide.

3. **311 Service Requests** — Already planned but not implemented. Slow 311 response times in lower-income neighborhoods are a well-documented pattern nationally; the data exists on Cincinnati's open data portal.

4. **Environmental Justice (Full Picture)** — We have flood risk but not the full EJ picture: no air quality (EPA EJScreen), no Superfund/brownfield proximity, no industrial emission data. Given Lower Price Hill's ecodistrict project and $800k in EPA remediation grants, this is high-profile.

5. **BRT Construction Context** — The Hamilton Ave and Reading Rd BRT corridors will reshape neighborhood access. Transit-adjacent neighborhoods will see pressure; residents need to understand the connection.
